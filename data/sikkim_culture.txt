Sikkim is a multiethnic and multilingual state in northeastern India. Nepali speakers make up the largest demographic group at around 62.6 percent of the population. Beyond Nepali, the state recognizes twelve official languages including Sikkimese (Bhutia), Lepcha, Limbu, Newar, Rai, Gurung, Magar, Sherpa, Tamang, Sunwar, and Bhujel. Indigenous Lepchas represent the earliest inhabitants, followed by Limbus and Magars in the western and southern districts, and later Bhutia settlers from Tibet. Nepali functions as the lingua franca while English and Hindi are widely used for communication across communities.

Hinduism is the predominant religion throughout Sikkim by number of adherents, while Vajrayana Buddhism holds deep cultural and historical significance, particularly among the Bhutia and Lepcha communities. Tradition holds that the Buddhist saint Padmasambhava (Guru Rinpoche) passed through the land in the 8th century and helped establish Buddhism in the region, an influence that shaped the state's many monasteries.

Major festivals observed across Sikkim include Losar (the Tibetan New Year, usually in February, marked by monastery mask dances and family gatherings), Saga Dawa (commemorating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha, typically in May or June, marked by processions and the lighting of butter lamps), Pang Lhabsol (a uniquely Sikkimese festival honoring Mount Khangchendzonga as the guardian deity of the state, usually in August or September), Bhumchu (the sacred water vase festival at Tashiding Monastery, in February or March), and Maghe Sankranti (a Nepali harvest and bathing festival in mid-January). Losoong, the Sikkimese/Bhutia New Year celebrated around December, is also widely observed with archery contests and folk dances.

Traditional dress varies by community: Bhutia men and women wear the "bakhu," a loose cloak-like garment, while Nepali communities favor the daura-suruwal for men and gunyu-cholo for women. Momos (steamed dumplings), thukpa (noodle soup), gundruk (fermented leafy greens), and sel roti (a traditional rice-flour bread) are staples of everyday Sikkimese food, alongside butter tea and chhaang, a fermented millet drink often served during festivals.

Sikkim became the first state in India to achieve full organic farming conversion, completing the transition between 2003 and 2016. Cardamom is the state's chief cash crop, and Sikkim produces more cardamom than any other Indian state. Rice, maize, millet, wheat, and citrus fruits are also widely cultivated, and this agricultural identity is closely tied to Sikkim's cultural practices and festivals tied to the harvest calendar.
