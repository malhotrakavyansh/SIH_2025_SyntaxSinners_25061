Dubdi Monastery holds the distinction of being the oldest monastery in Sikkim, established in 1701 by the ascetic Lhatsun Namkha Jigme. The name "Dubdi" derives from the local language meaning "the retreat," reflecting its origins as a hermit's cell. Three Tibetan lamas, including Lhatsun Namkha Jigme, gathered at Norbugang near Yuksom and crowned Phuntsog Namgyal as Sikkim's first king in 1642; the monastery was later constructed to commemorate this founding moment. Of four monasteries initially built during this era, Dubdi remains the sole survivor, making it exceptionally significant from a historical preservation standpoint.

The monastery is situated near Yuksom in the Geyzing subdivision of West Sikkim district. Perched atop a hillside at an elevation of about 2,100 meters, it requires roughly an hour's walk (about 3 kilometers, mostly uphill) from Yuksom to reach, and there is no vehicle access — visitors should wear comfortable walking shoes. Because it is a small, quiet site with a single resident monk, it is best visited earlier in the day, and advance notice may be needed to gain entry.

Dubdi Monastery belongs to the Nyingma sect of Tibetan Buddhism, the oldest school within Tibetan Buddhist traditions. The two-story stone structure follows a square floor plan, topped with a tapering tower and a bell-shaped gilded dome known as the "Gyaltshen." Interior spaces showcase elaborately painted surfaces housing religious statuary, including representations of the three founding lamas. The monastery preserves an extensive collection of rare manuscripts and ritual texts in its side aisles.

The monastery functions as a central node within a broader Buddhist pilgrimage network encompassing the Norbugang Chorten, Pemayangtse Monastery, Rabdentse ruins, Sanga Choeling Monastery, Khecheopalri Lake, and Tashiding Monastery. Its grounds also contain the Norbugang Coronation Throne and Kathok Lake, making the Yuksom area a natural base for a day or two of pilgrimage-circuit sightseeing before or after the Dzongri trek.
