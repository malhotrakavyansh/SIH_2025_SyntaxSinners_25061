Enchey Monastery holds a layered historical narrative that blends legend with documented establishment dates. According to local tradition, the site was blessed by Lama Druptab Karpo, a tantric Buddhist master renowned for his spiritual powers, who is said to have flown from Maenam Hill in South Sikkim to establish a small gompa at this location. The name "Enchey" translates to "Solitary Monastery," reflecting its original purpose as a contemplative retreat. The monastery as seen now was built in 1909 during the rule of Sikyong Tulku, though the eighth Chogyal had initiated monastery construction at the site in the 1840s. The structure was gutted by fire in 1947 but was reconstructed in 1948 with community support.

The monastery occupies a prominent ridge overlooking Gangtok, the capital of Sikkim, roughly 3 kilometers northeast of the main marketplace. The site commands spectacular views of the Kanchendzonga mountain range and is one of the easiest monasteries to reach for visitors already staying in Gangtok.

Enchey Monastery belongs to the Nyingma order of Vajrayana Buddhism and operates under the jurisdiction of Pemayangtse Monastery. Local belief holds that the protecting deities Khangchendzonga and Yabdean reside within the monastery, and legend attributes further sanctity to the site through associations with Guru Padmasambhava, who is said to have subdued powerful spirits here.

The monastery exemplifies Tibetan architectural style, crowned by a golden cupola. It is a double-storied stone masonry structure with thick walls and timber framing for columns, beams, and roofing. The prayer hall walls are covered with paintings and murals of the four religious kings and the deities of the four cardinal directions. The monastery currently houses around 90 monks.

The monastery hosts the Detor Cham, a colorful mask dance festival, on the 18th and 19th days of the 12th lunar month (typically January-February), featuring elaborate costumes depicting Tibetan deities including Mahakala and mythical snow lions. Pang Lhabsol, commemorating the blood-brotherhood between the Bhutia and Lepcha communities, is also observed here. These ceremonies occur shortly before Losar (Tibetan New Year) and Losoong (Sikkimese New Year). Because Enchey is close to central Gangtok, it is often visited together with the Gangtok Ropeway, the Directorate of Handicrafts and Handloom, and Ganesh Tok in a single day of sightseeing.
