Rumtek Monastery, also known as the Dharma Chakra Centre, was originally constructed in the mid-18th century under the direction of Changchub Dorje, the 12th Karmapa Lama. The monastery that exists today is a modern reconstruction. When Rangjung Rigpe Dorje, the 16th Karmapa, arrived in Sikkim in 1959 after fleeing Tibet, he discovered the original site in ruins. Despite being offered alternative locations, the Karmapa recognized the site's spiritual qualities, appreciating features such as flowing streams, mountains behind, a snow range in front, and a river below. With support from the Sikkim royal family and local residents, reconstruction was completed over four years. On Losar (Tibetan New Year) in 1966, the 16th Karmapa officially inaugurated the newly rebuilt monastery as his primary seat in exile.

The monastery is situated approximately 24 kilometers (15 miles) from Gangtok, the capital of Sikkim, at an elevation of about 1,500 meters (4,900 feet). Sikkim's mountainous setting complements the monastery's spiritual significance within the broader Himalayan Buddhist landscape.

Rumtek Monastery represents the Karma Kagyu school of Tibetan Buddhism and serves as the principal seat in exile of the Gyalwang Karmapa lineage. The monastery houses relics of the 16th Karmapa in a golden stupa and functions as a center for monastic practice and Buddhist scholarship. It currently stands as the largest monastery in Sikkim and contains the Karma Shri Nalanda Institute for Higher Buddhist Studies, where monks engage in rigorous theological education and spiritual training.

The monastery became the focal point of significant sectarian tensions within the Karma Kagyu tradition following the death of the 16th Karmapa. Two rival organizations emerged, each supporting a different candidate for the position of 17th Karmapa: the Tsurphu Labrang supporting Ogyen Trinley Dorje, and the Karmapa Charitable Trust supporting Trinley Thaye Dorje. Beginning in 1992, the monastery experienced pitched battles between monks supporting one candidate or the other. In 2003, Indian security forces intervened to remove monks supporting Trinley Thaye Dorje and restore order. Armed Indian military personnel have maintained a presence at the site since then to prevent further sectarian violence. Neither candidate has been enthroned at Rumtek itself.

The reconstructed monastery incorporates traditional Tibetan Buddhist architectural elements. The complex features a prominent golden stupa housing the 16th Karmapa's relics, prayer wheels for devotional practice, and ornate doors reflecting Himalayan Buddhist artistic traditions. The integrated Karma Shri Nalanda Institute provides facilities for advanced Buddhist studies alongside the main monastic spaces used for daily rituals and ceremonies.

The monastery welcomes visitors as one of Sikkim's primary tourist attractions. Its location near Gangtok makes it easily accessible for those touring the region, typically as a half-day trip. Visitors can observe monastic life, view religious artwork and artifacts, and experience the spiritual atmosphere of one of Tibetan Buddhism's most significant institutions outside of Tibet itself. Photography is generally permitted in the courtyard but may be restricted inside the main prayer hall, so visitors should check with monastery staff on arrival.
